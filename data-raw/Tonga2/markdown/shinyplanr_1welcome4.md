## The CARE principles that underpin conservation planning

Effective spatial planning is guided by four key principles: Connectivity, Adequacy, Representation, and Efficiency (CARE). Together, these ensure biodiversity is conserved over the long term while balancing human needs.

1. **Connectivity:** Connectivity is the movement of individuals, genes, energy, or materials among habitats or populations. Ensuring connectivity can mean safeguarding migration corridors, bird stopover sites, linked sites (e.g., nesting and feeding grounds), or source areas of coral or fish larvae.
2. **Adequacy:** Adequacy ensures that enough of each habitat or species is protected for long-term survival. Some species or ecosystems may require higher levels of protection to persist over time.
3. **Representation**: Representation is the cornerstone of conservation planning. It ensures that all biodiversity in a region — species, habitats, ecosystems, and key ecological processes — is captured and replicated across the protected area network.
4. **Efficiency**: Efficiency means achieving conservation goals while minimizing impacts on people and industries that rely on natural resources. Plans that reduce conflict are more likely to succeed. However, efficiency must be balanced carefully: "residual" areas with little human use may be easy to protect (low cost) but sometimes offer limited conservation value.
